# LinkedIn Profile Lead Generation Machine - 7 Changes

Your LinkedIn profile should be generating hundreds of qualified leads every single week. Your profile is the one thing that works for you 24 hours a day. Every post, every comment, every connection request points back to it. And most founders are wasting it. Hi, my name is Krystle Ali and 3 years ago I had 20,000 followers on LinkedIn. I was posting consistently, I was getting thousands of views and impressions, but I wasn't getting many leads at all. And today I have over 1.2 million followers and my LinkedIn profile is the main driver of leads and revenue for a portfolio of businesses. In this video, I'm going to show you seven changes that I made to turn my profile into a lead generation machine. So when the right person finds you, they instantly know what you do, who you help, and how to work with you.

## Step 1: Know Your ICP

So let's start with the one thing that most people skip entirely. That is knowing your ICP. Your ICP or your ideal customer profile is the specific person that you want to attract. And the trick here is to get really focused. You actually don't want your profile to appeal to 95% of people. You want it to be perfect for the 5% who have a specific problem that you can solve. So if you're an accountant, your ICP might be business owners who want to reduce their tax liability and pay their staff more efficiently. Or if you're a leadership coach, you're appealing to first-time managers in fast-growing companies. It all becomes incredibly clear once you understand who you're talking to, the language you should be using, and what appeals to that customer.

I often think about it this way. If I'm creating content for entrepreneurs who are interested in growth and scale, then everything that I post should sit within that world. And your profile should mirror that approach. Write down who your ideal customer is and then the 10 topics that they genuinely care about. And as a founder, this should be so deeply ingrained in you that it's best not to try and overthink this. And if you're not heavily involved in the day-to-day conversation with customers, you can ask Claude or ChatGPT and ask what the common challenges and interests of your ICP are. And have it summarize those into common themes for you. So three of these 10 things can often become your content pillars. But for the purposes of this video, we're going to include them in your profile.

## Step 2: Profile Photo

Now that you understand the fundamentals, let's build your LinkedIn profile starting with something that most people miss, the perfect profile photo. Your profile photo does one job and that is to make someone feel like they can trust you before they've read a single word that you have written. It's also worth noting that your profile picture is the thing that shows up every time that you comment on a post, so its potential is to reach millions of people. So it really does need to be right.

So let's start with the head shot. Just try and make sure that it's taken from the shoulders up with a decent natural light and a neutral background. If you can wear fairly plain clothes to put the focus on your face, that helps and don't over-edit it. You don't want it to look like AI. You also want to avoid things like group photos or selfies or distracting backgrounds or home settings or anything like that. The profile picture itself is 400 by 400 pixels. So make sure that you're taking a photo with that in mind. And if you really want to stand out, like I have on my profile, make the background a color, your brand color maybe or just one that stands out on the feed.

## Step 3: LinkedIn Banner

The other key visual is your LinkedIn banner. Most people treat this like an afterthought. There's a really big gap between what's possible and what most people are actually doing with their banners. So the worst thing that you can do is just select a photo that you like the look of because that doesn't communicate anything about who you are, what your business offers, or how you can help solve someone's specific problems.

So what does this actually look like? The technical specs of the banner are 1584 by 396 pixels. So that's the space that you have to play with, but there's actually three things that your banner needs to do.

First, you need to set the tone. So try and use consistent colors between your company, your content, and your banner. And these brand colors begin to become recognizable between you and your content, so you have to get them right.

Second, you need to have a way to communicate one clear value proposition. If we think back to the leadership coach example, it could be, "I help new leaders become excellent managers using a four-step process." So what you need to get across is who you help and what outcome you're going to deliver to that person. And that is why it's so important that we properly understand our ICP.

Finally, the third thing your banner needs to include is a call to action. Specifically, if this is the only thing that they see, what do you want them to do? So don't try and hard sell here. The aim is to push them into a funnel. Say what you do and tell them to look at that featured section.

## Step 4: LinkedIn Headline

The next part is the single most important part of your profile. This is your LinkedIn headline. And not many people focus on this, but the headline will follow you around LinkedIn and it will appear next to anything that you comment on, anything that you post, anything that you interact with. And so when you're writing it, you really need to give people an understanding of who you are and what you do.

So most people just write their job title, but unless you work at a big blue-chip company like Apple, Google, or Goldman Sachs, that doesn't really mean anything to anyone. So the formula that I teach, and I'll encourage you to use this, is "I am a insert your role that helps a specific audience, your ICP we talked about earlier, achieve a desired result." So when you spell it out like that, you're leaving no doubt of exactly what you can offer and who you help. And this really gives you credibility in your audience's mind.

So my headline today is co-founder of Searchable. I help ambitious businesses drive revenue from AI search. And when I started my first company, when I was running a digital marketing agency in my 20s and it specialized in luxury brands, my headline was "Founder of digital marketing agency Verb Brands helping luxury brands attract high-net-worth individuals."

See, you've got to remember, the purpose is to make your ideal customer immediately self-identify themselves. And the moment they read your headline, they should be thinking, "You know what? That is exactly what I need." Or even, "Actually, this person's definitely not for me." So for you, both of those outcomes are wins because you don't want to be spending time trying to convince someone to buy something if they were never actually going to do it in the first place.

So one more thing to note, you have 220 characters available for this. So try and keep it to two lines max. Most people aren't going to stop and read a long headline.

## Step 5: Bio / About Section

Your headline sells what you do, but there's one section that sells who you are and that is your bio. So the bio or the about section is the part of the profile that offers the most space to freely write and explain why people should follow you. Most people use this space to write out their CV or their life story, but neither of those things really work.

So here's the right way to do it. By the time someone reaches your bio, they're already interested in who you are and what you have to say. Now they're looking to understand specifically, can you as a person and as a service provider specifically solve the problem that they have? So when faced with this, most people will lean heavily towards credentials. But if they're interested in reading your bio, they probably already know most of your credentials. So try and lean the other way. Showcase some of the character and what you can do for them specifically that stands you out from the crowd.

So explain what transformation you can give them and what role you can play in helping them achieve their objectives. Then try and share relevant parts of your story that will help them make their decision. So for me, a lot of the work that I do is helping experienced entrepreneurs grow their businesses often by how to reach new audiences. I've done it for 16 years across three or four different big companies. And so when it comes to my story, I list out nine bullet points what I was doing at each age because I want people to know the depth and the breadth of my experience.

So don't overcomplicate it with small details and how you did something. You can save that for later down your funnel. Keep the sentences short with line breaks fairly often and write at a level that someone could read whilst they're on the train or the commute to work.

## Step 6: Custom Buttons and Featured Section

This is the section that most people completely ignore. If you do this correctly, you will turn a casual visitor into an actual lead that is interested in your business.

So let's start with custom buttons. When I teach people how to do this, I'm always surprised at how few people know these actually exist. But it's a really strong tool that you can use because every time you post a piece of content, this custom button will show up creating the opportunity for someone to get in touch with you. And that is potentially thousands of times that you could push people straight into your funnel every single day.

So my personal advice is to direct this through to whatever is the most important part of your business or the best lead magnet that you have in your business. So if you have a webinar coming up, drive people to that. If you're running a sales event, push people into that side of the business. But whatever it is, make sure that you are sending them to a page that will at some point ask for their email and gives them an offer that is so good that they don't hesitate to give you their email. So whether it's a free guide on your subject matter, a Notion template, a custom GPT on your topic, videos they can access elsewhere, or a newsletter, something that's worth trading their email for.

So next up is one of my favorite parts of the profile because it's often the part that drives the warmest leads to your business. And that is your featured section. It's right in the middle of your profile. And there are a lot of people that don't know that this even exists or how to optimize it. They leave it completely empty. Or if they do know about it, they often fill it with random previous successful LinkedIn posts that have performed well, which in my opinion is honestly a huge waste of opportunity.

Remember, you are designing this profile for your ICP. And they don't care if one of your posts has gone viral previously or got 1,000 likes. They're interested in you and what you do and how you can help them. And this is the section where you can give them somewhere to go. But you should always try and have two clear offers.

First you should always have a direct link to your actual offer, whether it's an inquiry form or a product sign up. By the time someone reaches this point on your profile, they're going to be highly interested in what you have to offer. Now, the second path is to capture those that are less warm and need more convincing but still are interested in what you do at this stage. So, for this audience, the best thing to do is to give them an irresistible offer and push them into a newsletter sequence.

The problem you may face is that some people are still guarded about giving up their personal information. So, I like to focus here on creating high-value lead magnets. Lead magnets are very valuable resources that contain a condensed version of your offer or data that your ICP is deeply interested in. So, what this does is create a value exchange between you and your ICP. You get their email address and in exchange they receive a very valuable piece of information that they can immediately put into practice.

A general rule of thumb here - if you are giving away a lead magnet and you are exchanging it for an email, I want the ICP customer to feel as if they would have happily paid for that piece of work that they've received. And that for me is a great yardstick as to whether a lead magnet is truly valuable and whether they will walk away with a positive experience from it.

As a bonus tip, if you want to get really detailed and optimize every single part of the real estate on your profile, even under your job role, you can add a media element that creates a link and you can have it direct to a lead magnet. If you look at my profile, you will see that I am the co-founder of Searchable and underneath the description of my job, I have a media element that says a free visibility report. And if someone clicks that, they immediately go into a lead magnet and immediately trade their email for use of my product.

## Step 7: De-Platforming - Own Your Audience

Now, all of this work that we've been doing on optimizing our profile is pointless if you aren't able to develop a strong infrastructure around it. So, everything we've discussed so far is about getting attention on LinkedIn, getting attention on social media. The biggest limiting factor of LinkedIn and social media in general is that you don't control how successful you are within the algorithm.

You can create the best content in the world that will appeal to the most people. However, if we are posting content that is directly pushing our offer, a case study, a testimonial, those posts are bottom of the funnel. Those posts typically don't do particularly well because they don't relate to that many people. And so, as a result, LinkedIn and other social media platforms will look at that post and determine that it is not a successful post and doesn't push it out.

Social media is an amazing avenue to drive attention and to get customers to know about you and to get exposure for your business. The thing you have to do is make sure that you actually own your audience. And so, the audience you have on social media, you don't own. You don't control that. You can't determine how many people see your content.

And so, the solution is what I call de-platforming. And de-platforming, as the name suggests, is about using your LinkedIn content to pull people off LinkedIn and onto something that you actually own the distribution of. So, for me, that is my newsletter, which has about 300,000 subscribers now. And every Sunday morning, I send them an email. And I know that there is no algorithm that sits between me sending it and them receiving it.

So, the lead magnet strategy we discussed in step six is a great way to de-platform your audience. So, I'd suggest that you commit some serious effort in developing a resource that people want to download. This could be a framework. It could be a resource. It could be a report. Just make it genuinely useful for your ICP. And once they download it, they subscribe to your email and therefore become a contact that you can market to in future.

I run a company called Searchable. And at Searchable, our lead magnet is effectively a free visibility report for your company. So, you can look at how your company is performing in ChatGPT, in Gemini, in Claude. So, you can see how much traffic you're generating from AI. Now, because of the way we set up our lead magnet, people sign up for the tool and immediately get an in-depth report and an agent they can use to do strategy and work out how to improve their search, all for trading their email. Now, we're able to put them into our product. And the most amazing thing about a lead magnet when done right is that about 39% of people who sign up for a free account go on to then become customers of our tool.

### Qualifying Questions for Your Lead Magnet

When building your lead magnet, ask three qualifying questions:

1. The type of business that they are
2. The size of business they are (how many employees or what revenue)
3. What budget they have to achieve this specific thing

By asking those three specific questions, you know exactly who your customer is and what problems they have. As a result, when you're marketing in future and you're sending out emails, you don't send someone the wrong offer. Let's say that you deliver 10,000 pound consultancy services, but you also deliver 1,000 pounds digital product. You don't want to send the beginner the 10,000 pound offer. You just want to send the beginner the 1,000 pound offer.
