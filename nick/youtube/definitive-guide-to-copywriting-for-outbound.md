## The Psychology of Saying Yes to a Message from a Stranger

The first and most important thing — copywriting is not magic. Everything I'm about to show you has actually been documented in psychological literature for the better part of several hundred years. You can go out right now and verify that there have been experiments and tests done on different groups of people just by looking it up on a resource like ArXiv.

I went to school for behavioral neuroscience. I graduated with a degree that basically discusses and shows the neuroscientific basis behind why human beings make decisions, why we engage in specific behaviors. The best cold email copywriters are basically the best psychologists.

### The Seven Principles

There are seven principles behind why people say yes to messages from strangers. A lot of this was taken from Robert Cialdini's book called Influence.

### 1. Give First

This is why before you get your bill at a restaurant, the waiter or waitress will always bring you a pile of breath mints. When they provide you something that you think is valuable, even if it's not objectively valuable, it will create a sense of obligation that lowers their resistance, disarms their skepticism, and ultimately opens the door to trust.

In our case for cold email, the big hurdle is trust. You start by giving something really small and then that gets you a foot in the door where you can start a conversation. In the conversation you give them something else which gets you a foot in the door to where maybe you could send a proposal. Then you consistently increase the level of escalation until ultimately they have some sort of business relationship with you.

It really boils down to offering some sort of help that seems genuine. It might involve giving them insights: "Hey, I noticed XYZ is currently misconfigured on your landing page. I think you're losing between $10 to $20,000 a month. Let me show you how to fix this." You do that without asking for anything in return. And even though you didn't make the ask, the ask is inferred.

### 2. Micro Commitments

This is that sense of increasing escalation where you start with one thing which is small, use that as your foot in the door for another thing which is bigger, and so on. In any sort of outbound situation, you can't just say "Hey, do you want to pay me $4,000 right now?"

Instead: "Hey, I actually recorded you this custom thing and it walks you through how to do all this value stuff. Would you just watch it for one minute? If even after one minute you just don't think it's valuable, never message me again." If they watch that minute and they like it, they're already assenting to engaging in some sort of transaction with you. From there you can turn that into a longer video, then an actual phone call, then a video call, then ultimately getting them to close.

The human brain is very leaky — there are all these little biases and hacks. The more times you can get a person to say yes before the ask, the higher the probability of them actually saying yes to the ask.

### 3. Social Proof

Human beings are herd consensus animals. We make decisions first by looking at a bunch of other people and seeing whether they're making the same decision before we say yes to anything.

**How to do this in practice:**
- Use very specific numbers — concrete data like names, results, counts
- "Hey, I actually just finished a client project for an IT business in Tennessee as well. They're a 15-minute drive from you. I can hook you up with them if you want."
- "We generated $112,482 last week for an XYZ business"
- Match the reference group — your results should always be in the context of the person you're pitching to

The closer the Venn diagram overlap between your reference and your prospect (characteristics, businesses, ICP attributes), the better the social proof will land.

### 4. Authority

Demonstrate hyper-relevant expertise with some form of credentialism, renowned accomplishment, or confidence in the way you write.

- Don't hedge every 5 seconds. Not "Well, I believe maybe I could help you." Instead: "Hey, I could absolutely 100% help you. I'm very confident in this because I just helped XYZ do this before."
- Make sure the credibility matches the ICP you're trying to talk to
- There are tons of really simple partnership programs and incentive programs you can sign up to that give you authority even if you don't have any in the niche

### 5. Rapport

Find shared context between you and the person you're attempting to pitch — ethnic, cultural, career shared context, or even that you both have Yorkshire terriers.

Two types:
- **Explicit**: Directly stating shared context — "Hey, I know you got a Yorkshire terrier"
- **Implicit**: Mirroring communication styles, message lengths, punctuation. If pitching San Francisco VCs in 2022, you'd use lowercase in outbound messages to signal you're part of their group.

### 6. Scarcity

Limit the availability (number of things you have to give) or create time pressure (give them a deadline).

- "That proposal expires at the end of the week"
- Make the constraint real — fake constraints trigger marketing BS detectors
- Real genuine ones: your own personal capacity, your own schedule, the fact that you're juggling additional client projects

This is probably the least commonly used principle.

### 7. Shared Identity

Establish common ground. People within your industry, who share specific values, who have gone through similar challenges or hardships.

- Use in-group language (like lowercase messages for tech culture)
- Highlight some form of shared struggle
- Mirror the tonality and length of messages

You don't have to memorize every single one of these. Just recognize what they look like. If you can get in the habit of recognizing these patterns, you can learn to employ them yourself.

## Three Components of a Successful Outbound Campaign

### 1. Establishing Clear Goals

Every single email or SMS should function as a self-contained campaign. The way I always think of my goals: **what is the one thing I want them to do after reading my message?**

Possible goals:
- **Reply** — easiest, most common
- **Watch something** — bigger ask since they're clicking a link
- **Book a call** — much tougher but closer to the end goal
- **Buy something** — golden grail but rare in practice

If your goal is a reply, you'll write very differently than if your goal is to book a call. Minimize the number of steps the person has to take. If going for a book-a-call: "Hey, can I give you a ring at 2:15? I have your phone number right here."

**If you can't describe your goal in one sentence, you're not ready to write your campaign.**

### 2. Building a Frame

The frame of cold outbound is one-to-one comms. The entire way you get those psychological principles on paper is you write as if the person thinks you're writing just to them.

**The text message test:** If a friend saw you typing this to them, would they think it was a personal message or a mass email? Optimize for personal.

**Kill corporate signals:**
- No "hope this finds you well"
- No illustrious signature blocks
- Don't say "we" — say "I"
- Short, casual, slightly imperfect, like a real person having a conversation
- Include "sent from my iPhone" at the bottom
- Even purposefully insert minor issues to make it seem real

### 3. Iteration

What makes a great outbound campaign is having goals + frame + time + applying changes based on data.

The scientific method for outbound:
1. Come up with a hypothesis ("I think this email would help me book calls")
2. Send it
3. Evaluate results
4. Apply feedback to improve
5. Loop over and over

Build a funnel with all your metrics: opens → replies → opt-ins → calls → proposals → closed → lifetime value. Kill losers fast. After you've sent enough, cut bottom performers, write new variants based on top performers, test again.

**Data over gut feeling.** I can't tell you how many times I've written a campaign and thought "There's no way this would work" and then it gets a 15% reply rate and generates hundreds of thousands of dollars.

## The Four-Step Copywriting Framework ($15M Framework)

### Step 1: Personalization

The highest ROI place in an entire cold email is always the very beginning. Email drop-off is nuts — if the first word is at 100% of people, the fifth word is down to 50%.

**The personalization is composed of:**
1. A greeting
2. An observation or thing in common (handles rapport + in-group signaling)
3. A segue into your pitch

**Rules:**
- Cannot signal that you are selling something
- Two sentences max. One sentence is ideal.
- Would a real person send this? If yes, you have a strong opener.

**Bad example:** "Hey Stacy, love how passionate you are about process optimization and aligning corporations with diversity outcomes at BeaverCorp."

**Good example:** "Hey Stacy, saw you went to UCLA. That's wild. My cousin went down there."

The entire point of personalization is for the person to go: "Wait, who is that? Do I know this person?" That buys you 30 seconds of them reading through the rest of your email.

#### Cold Reading

A set of psychological techniques used to convince someone that you know them intimately even if you've never met them. Make generalized statements that apply to 80% of the population that a person upon first read would not know applies to 80% of the population.

**Example:** "Hey Nick, love your channel, man. Very no BS and has helped me get started in management consulting. Think I can help you with something and maybe return a bit of the favor you've unwittingly done me."

You could send this to any YouTuber making business content. "Management consulting" is so general that any business model would apply. Everybody thinks their channel is "very no BS."

**Voluntary disclosure of information:** When you voluntarily disclose some bit of information about yourself ("helped me get started in management consulting"), the person on the other end will trust you more because you gave them some personal info. This is a common tactic used by the FBI.

### Step 2: Who Am I (Identity)

The sequence when somebody reads a cold email:
1. First thought: "Is this person a scammer/spammer?" → Personalization solves this
2. Second thought: "Who are they and why should I give a shit?" → This step solves this

Keep it to one or two sentences. The social proof is your introduction.

**Best template:** "I currently work with [insert client — name or 'industry client in location'] to help them do [thing similar to what they've done]. We've done [specific number] in [time period]."

This knocks out:
- Social proof (showing people why you matter)
- In-group alignment (similar company to theirs)
- Authority (impressive results)

**Example:** "I currently work with a 10 mil sub YouTuber, Mr. X, to help him build landing pages. We have made $3 mil in the last month alone."

### Step 3: The Offer

This is where you make them an offer so good that saying no feels pretty irrational. Point out something specific about their situation — an observation, pain point, or need.

**The offer template:** I will do [X thing] in [Y time] or [Z risk mitigation].

**Examples:**
- "I will generate you $10,000 in 60 days or I'll keep working for free until I do"
- "I will get you 20 calls in 90 days or I'll give you all of your money back"

The whole idea is it needs to sound really good, really clean, be super quantified, be hyper specific. No ranges. Not "I'll make you between 10 to 20k." Instead: "I'll make you 20k in 90 days."

**The offer formula:**

```
Conversion Rate = (Return on Investment × Trust that you'll fulfill) / Friction in implementation
```

When the numerator is high and the denominator is low, conversion rate is high.

#### Scaling Offers to Income Levels

Your offers should be correlated to the incomes and statuses of the businesses you're working with. If you're pitching YouTubers making $5-10M/year and your offer is $100K additional revenue — that's only a 2% improvement in their business effectiveness. Very achievable.

### Step 4: Call to Action (CTA)

Make a specific ask. No vague stuff like "Would you be interested?" or "Let me know your thoughts."

**Template:** "Would you be open to a 15-minute chat? If so, I can give you a ring at 3:30 PM today or before 12:00 PM tomorrow."

One step between yes and booked. Every additional back-and-forth leaks ~5% of your funnel. If you're an average person, that's 25% of your whole lead flow disappearing.

**Recap the four steps:**
1. Personalization — gets them to say "you're not a spammer"
2. Who am I / Why do I matter — tells them who you are
3. Offer — what can you do for me
4. CTA — specific ask with specific time

Do not try bending or breaking the rules until you understand them. 80-90% of all situations will fall within this formula.

## Offer Construction Deep Dive

### The Offer Formula Restated

```
Conversion Rate = (ROI × Trust you'll fulfill) / Friction in implementation
```

**Return on Investment:** Must be quantified, clear, and time-bound.
- "I'll book you 20 meetings in 60 days" ✓
- "I'll book you some meetings" ✗
- "I'll book you meetings" ✗

**Trust:** Built through rapport, social proof, in-group alignment, and authority. If you're an authority figure, highly credentialed, the probability they think you'll deliver is higher.

**Friction:** Make it really easy to start.
- "I'll just need 15 minutes of your time over a brief call once at the beginning"
- "How does 3:30 PM today sound? I can give you a ring at [number] or send you a one-click Google Meet invite"
- Risk mitigation: "If I don't generate you 20 meetings in 60 days, you don't pay a cent"

### Why Offers 3x Your Top of Funnel

Before offer: 100% (baseline revenue)
After offer with ~10% margin cost for unfulfilled guarantees: 270% revenue = 2.7x total profit

### Example Offers by Industry

**Lead Generation:** "I will guarantee you 20 booked sales appointments in the next 60 days or you don't pay, no strings."

**Live Chat Software:** "I'll build a world-class live chat widget on your website at no cost. I won't charge you anything until you get your first 10 paying clients. Making this completely risk-free."

**Proposal System:** "I will build you the same high-converting proposal template that's made [company] $5 mil in the last 2 months. I'll do all the work up front. Only if you like it will I ask you to work with me. Just say yes and I'll send you yours within 48 hours."

**SEO Audit:** "I'll run a full SEO audit on your site. Show you exactly which pages are losing you traffic. Completely free. Just say yes. I'll have it in your inbox within 24 hours."

**Google Ads:** "I'll build you a Google Ads campaign that I guarantee will generate 50 qualified leads in 30 days. You don't pay a cent. Just say yes and I'll get it live this week."

**Content Writing:** "Just send us a title and we'll give you a completely free 500-word blog post on the topic. No strings attached."

**CRM:** "I will build you a world-class [industry] CRM at no cost. I'll pay for it all myself. Do all the work up front and only if you like it will I ask you to work with me."

**Web Design:** "I'll redesign your homepage for free. Send you a mockup within 72 hours. You only pay if you absolutely love it."

### Systems vs Templates

A template works really well then stops working because the market gets used to it. A system can produce a million different templates. The strategy is the system. The tactic is the template. Focus on higher-level strategy.

## Platform-Specific Optimization

### Cold Email — What You Can Optimize

1. **Name of the sender** (~20 characters) — Use full name, first piece of info people see
2. **Subject line** (~30-50 characters) — Something a friend might send, informal, plausible deniability
3. **Teaser** (~50-100 characters) — First preview text, combined with subject line totals ~150 characters
4. **Profile picture** — Professional, clear
5. **Email address** — Signals legitimacy
6. **Email body** — The actual copy

Any unused space in subject line and teaser gets filled with metadata. Aim for 150 characters combined minimum.

### LinkedIn

- **Profile picture** — Professional corporate photo, stands out with distinct background
- **Name** — Shorter first name = more teaser space
- **Title/Job title** (~50-60 characters) — Visible immediately
- **LinkedIn Premium badge** — Gold badge, makes you stand out
- **Credentials** — Certifications, partner badges (like "HubSpot Elite Partner")
- **Message body**

**High-level advice:** Have a professional corporate profile picture. Get premium plus certifications to add more to the top of your message body and decrease probability of landing in "Other" mailbox.

### X (Twitter)

- **Profile picture**
- **Full name** (may not be real name)
- **Teaser** (~45-55 characters)
- **X tag/handle** — Matters for credibility
- **Join date** — New accounts look spammy
- **Message body** — Not truncated, full content visible

**High-level advice:** The whole game is making it out of requests. Write casually in the same tone of voice that most other X users use.

### Instagram

- **Profile picture**
- **Handle** (~30 characters in teaser)
- **Name** (can differ from handle)
- **Platform source** (Facebook, Instagram, etc.)
- **Message body**

**High-level advice:** The whole game is making it out of message requests. Write casually.

### iMessage/SMS

- **Profile picture**
- **Phone number**
- **Message teaser** (~90 characters)
- **Message body**

**High-level advice:** Fill out the entire teaser. Insert something provocative near the end to ensure a click.

### Universal Tip

Across all platforms, insert something provocative or curiosity-inducing near the end of visible preview text to ensure they click into the full message.

## Subject Lines

### The Core Principle: Plausible Deniability

The entire point of the subject line is NOT to tell them everything about your email upfront. You want to give them enough information to pique their curiosity, but not enough to fully satisfy their need without clicking.

**You do not sell in the subject line.** If you find yourself selling at all in the subject line, you're doing something wrong.

### Bad Subject Lines

- "Interested in AI-driven performance optimization" — Gives away the whole pitch
- "Video editor" — Nothing custom, nothing that begs a question
- "Nick you scaled to 72K a month with automation. Here's the next level." — Too long, blatantly selling
- No subject at all — Correlated with spam

### Good Subject Lines

- "Your Claude Code course and a formatting thought" — Lowercase, personal, references specific content
- "2026 capital" — Could mean a million things (investment? Acquisition?)
- "Been with you from the start" — Casual, human, curiosity-inducing
- "Nick's blind spot" — Fear of loss, must click to find out
- "Nick, you're wasting $2,300 per month" — Loss aversion, specific number
- "Nick Q" — Super simple, seems personal
- "Nick, are you taking coaching clients?" — Sounds like a buyer, not a seller
- "Nick, are you hiring?" — Same principle

### Why Loss-Based Subjects Work

Human beings respond much better to any sort of accusation of loss than they do to potential upside. "You're wasting $2,300/month" is more compelling than "I can make you $2,300/month."

## Follow-Ups

### Keep It Simple

I typically only send two emails initially — the initial email and one follow-up. The follow-ups don't have to be complicated.

**Good follow-up:** "Hey, X, checking in on Y. TL;DR, if this is the first time you're seeing this: [brief email body]. Thanks, Nick."

**Bad follow-up:** "Peter, while analyzing Leftclick, I noticed you were spending XYZ on platforms you didn't have to be. Our recent case study, AAA Corp, saved over $10,000 by doing whatever." Real humans don't talk like this.

**Real human follow-ups:**
- "Hey, Pete, are you cool, man? Get back to me."
- "Hey, Pete, just checking in on XYZ. Let me know if you have any questions."
- "Hey, Pete. Let me know if this got lost in your mailbox."

### Why Only Two Initially

You want to minimize the proportion of people that mark you as spam. Multiple emails in a sequence when you're not sure it's good increases block risk. Start with two emails. When your campaign starts overperforming, add a third step — it'll lift performance while minimizing health risk to your assets (mailboxes, phone numbers, profiles).

Adding email 3 to a 4.8% reply rate campaign might take it to 6.1%. Adding email 4 might take it to 6.8%. But you need to hit a minimum bar first.

## Iteration

### The Reality

Your cold outbound campaigns rarely rock right away. They usually suck. You start at ~2.5%, make changes, get to 3.2%, maybe dip back to 3%, then climb to 4%, and gradually plateau at your true reply rate for that campaign.

### Rules for Iteration

1. **Always iterate.** Always have multiple variants going simultaneously. No matter what, always be sending more than one variant.

2. **Stick to a schedule.** I iterate every Sunday for 20-30 minutes. Make minor adjustments to emails, change subject lines, etc. Per year, that's 45-50 iteration cycles that nobody else gets.

3. **500-1,000 per variant.** You need enough volume for statistical significance. If you've only sent 50-100, any decision you make is basically random. 100 emails with zero replies doesn't mean it's bad — you don't have enough data to know.

4. **Pick a TAM with a lot of leads.** Total addressable market. Holistic nutritionists in Texas = a couple hundred leads (can't even do one good test). Digital agencies in the US = 100,000 leads (100-200 tests possible).

5. **Make big changes early and small changes late.** When starting out, test two fundamentally different scripts. Short vs long. Formal vs informal. Personalized vs generic. Quickly cut half the possibilities that don't work. Then progressively make smaller and smaller changes — the 3x difference becomes 2x, then 1x, then 0.5x — until you've narrowed in on a really high performer where you're just tweaking words and subject lines.

## AI in Copywriting

### The Honest Take

I rarely if at all use AI in my copywriting these days. Personalization does not replace a good quality campaign. AI is not yet at the ability where it can meaningfully craft very persuasive copy.

The skill required to get to reasonable competence in copywriting is actually pretty low — about 2 weeks to reach 75% of total available skill. But the gap between "good" (what AI can write) and "great" takes years. There's almost a skill floor where if you're not better than what AI can do, nobody's even opening your emails.

Across over 10,000 people in Maker School actively doing this for their business: **people that use AI really intensely in the copywriting process tend to suck.**

### Three Legitimate Uses of AI in Copywriting

**1. Small templated variables that fit into a pre-established already working template.**

Don't use AI to write the whole email. Use it for the personalization snippet only:
- Feed AI their LinkedIn data → output which web property they're known for
- "Love your [channel/LinkedIn/blog], man. Big fan. Has made me think a lot about [something they're involved in]."

Or even simpler — just one variable: the casual version of their company name.

**2. Casualization of company names, neighborhoods, schools, or other rapport-building approaches.**

- "Leftclick Incorporated" → "Leftclick"
- "The Pacific Creative Group LLC" → "PCG"
- "Vancouver, British Columbia" → "East Van"

AI has enough geographical and cultural knowledge to casualize properly. This signals you're part of their in-group without manual research.

**3. Scrape leads and enrich info.**

Use AI for data gathering and enrichment, not for writing.

## Advanced Grey Hat Outbound Techniques

**Disclaimer:** These techniques trade compliance with local laws/regulations and platform terms of service. I can't explicitly recommend any of them. Use at your own risk.

### 1. Buying Pre-Warmed Social Media Accounts + Email

There are vendors where you can buy pre-warmed LinkedIn accounts, Instagram accounts, X accounts, and email mailboxes. These have organic usage history so platforms are less likely to ban them and they may have higher outbound limits.

**Pre-warmed mailboxes** (available on Instantly, Smartlead, etc.) are essentially fake personas — first name, last name, domain name that may not exist. You'll need to explain the discrepancy if you get on a call.

**LinkedIn profile renting:** Some companies hire people in lower cost-of-living nations and use their LinkedIn accounts for outbound. Very slimy, but it's happening.

### 2. Power Dialers for Cold Calling

A power dialer parallelizes calls — instead of calling sequentially (30 seconds dial time each), you call 3+ numbers simultaneously and connect with whoever picks up first. This effectively 2-2.5x your talk time efficiency.

Some platforms also do automatic voicemail drops — pre-record a message and drop it to 50,000 phone numbers without actually calling. There are regulations against this.

### 3. Cold SMS/WhatsApp via Third-Party APIs

Platforms that emulate iPhone-to-iPhone (blue bubble) sending behavior even when sending campaigns through a web interface. Also cold SMS platforms (like Instantly for email but for SMS), WhatsApp, Telegram, and Viber.

Cold outbound messaging is extremely regulated. These platforms skirt regulations by distributing high volume across many numbers in parallel.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The seven psychological principles:** Give first, micro commitments, social proof, authority, rapport, scarcity, shared identity
2. **The four-step copywriting framework:** Personalization → Who am I → Offer → CTA
3. **The offer formula:** CVR = (ROI × Trust) / Friction
4. **Cold reading** is the most underutilized personalization technique
5. **Systems beat templates** — learn the system, produce infinite templates
6. **Iterate constantly** — big changes early, small changes late, 500-1000 per variant
7. **Use AI sparingly** — only for small templated variables and casualization
8. **Optimize every element** — sender name, subject line, teaser, profile picture, email address, body
9. **Subject lines don't sell** — they create plausible deniability to earn a click
10. **Minimize steps** — every back-and-forth leaks ~5% of your funnel
