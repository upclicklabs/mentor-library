# Full LinkedIn Playbook from Zero - The Complete System

22 years old, living in Colombia, fired from an SEO agency, no clients, no network, no savings, no plan. By 25, made a million dollars without ads, without a big following, without posting on five different platforms - just one. LinkedIn.

## Why LinkedIn

LinkedIn has 1.3 billion members, but only 3% post weekly. Like a stadium with 50,000 seats where everyone's in the stands but almost nobody's on the field. When you show up on that field, every person in those stands can see you. You don't need to be the best - you just need to be there.

The audience isn't there to be entertained. They're executives, founders, CMOs - people with budgets and a problem they need solved this quarter. When someone on LinkedIn sees your content and thinks "I need this," they can act on it the same day. That's a completely different buying environment than any other platform.

## Why One Platform

The "be everywhere" advice sounds right on paper. But:
- You become generic - content that works everywhere works nowhere
- You burn out managing five platforms
- You never build intuition for any single algorithm
- Learning an algorithm is like learning a city - you can't know five cities deeply at once

The question isn't "which platforms should I be on?" The question is: where are my buyers and how do I own that channel before everyone else does?

For B2B, LinkedIn is not social media - it's a distribution channel. I ignored every other platform until I hit a specific revenue milestone.

## Understanding the Algorithm: 360 Brew

LinkedIn published a research paper written by their own engineers explaining how they rebuilt the algorithm from scratch. They call it 360 Brew.

The old system worked like a committee - modules tracking trending topics, hashtags, early engagement velocity. All of them voting on what to show people. That's gone.

Replaced by a single AI model. 150 billion parameters, built in-house, trained entirely on LinkedIn's own data. One brain, not a committee.

**What changed:**
- Old system matched keywords. New one reads meaning.
- It's asking whether your post actually belongs in front of a specific person based on everything it knows about them
- It tracks whether people stopped and actually read what you wrote - not just liked it
- It knows the difference between someone who really commented vs. someone who typed "great insight" without reading a single word

**The algorithm now favors smaller accounts.** LinkedIn ran AB tests - for accounts with smaller networks, the new algorithm produced measurably better outcomes. Data from 50,000 posts published in late 2025: a top 10% post from someone with 5K to 10K followers gets around 5.5K impressions. A medium post from someone with 25K to 50K followers gets 2.4K. A great post from a smaller account outperforms an average post from an account five times its size.

**What stopped working:**
- Timing optimization
- Hashtags (the system rewards meaning now, not labels)
- Engagement pods (360 Brew tracks dwell time and genuine engagement)
- Generic advice - when thousands post the same five tips, you're all in the same lane

**What it rewards now:**
- Genuine expertise
- Content people actually dwell on
- Saves over likes
- A voice that sounds like a real human with real expertise

## LinkedIn SEO

LinkedIn is the number two most cited source in AI overviews. Your profile is being read by the algorithm as a context signal - 360 Brew reads your profile before distributing any of your content. It's asking: based on who this person says they are, does this post belong in front of that audience?

**What moves the needle:**
- Headline needs keywords, not just titles. Not "SEO consultant helping B2B SaaS teams grow" - try "SEO and AEO consultant for B2B SaaS" because it matches what buyers actually search for
- Services section is searchable gold - fill it in with the exact language your ICP uses
- About section as a sales page - write with natural keywords the way your buyer would describe their own problem
- Post consistently around core keywords - the system builds a content fingerprint over time
- Name your carousel files before uploading - they tell the system what the document is about
- Add alt text to images - the system actually reads it

## Profile as a Landing Page

Your content is the ad. Your profile is the landing page. If the ad is great and the landing page is confusing, nobody buys.

**The funnel:** Someone sees your post → clicks your profile → scans your headline → checks your featured section. That's literally a funnel. Treat it like one.

- **Banner:** Your re-hook. Gets them to keep moving down the page.
- **Headline:** Your value proposition, not your job title. Use the exact language your buyers search for.
- **About section:** Problem, solution, proof, CTA. Not your life story. Nobody's scrolling LinkedIn to read your autobiography.
- **Featured section:** Goal is to move people one step closer into your world - somewhere you own the relationship. Newsletter sign-up, booking link, lead magnet. LinkedIn owns your followers, you don't.
- **Custom CTA button:** Link it to a booking page, newsletter, or download. Don't leave it blank.

Spend one full day on this before you post anything.

## 72 Hours of Landscape Study

Before posting, spend 72 hours studying the landscape. Reference: "Steal Like an Artist."

The bad musician learns one song and plays it note for note. The good musician listens to 50 artists, absorbs what makes each one resonate, and writes something that sounds like nobody else.

Find accounts in your space with consistently strong engagement from your ICP. Don't base this on follower count - base it on post performance. A 3,000 follower account with strong engagement from your ICP is more valuable to study than a 100K account whose comments are all from other creators.

Study them:
- What topics do they keep coming back to?
- What hooks stop people from scrolling?
- What formats get traction?
- How do they end their posts?
- What are the outliers and why?

You are not doing this to copy them - you are doing this to understand the unwritten rules of your niche before you walk in. Steal formats, hooks, design frameworks - not ideas.

After 6 months, something shifts. You stop noticing what's working for everyone else and start noticing what's missing - what your niche keeps getting wrong, what nobody is saying but everyone needs to hear. That's when you stop stealing and start building something that's actually yours.

## The Content System: Four Buckets

Random posting doesn't work. Think of your content like a sales team where every person has a different job.

### Growth Content (40%)

The authority paradox: the more you know, the more you assume people will care. But nobody cares until you earn their attention first. Expertise and attention are two completely different currencies. You can be rich in one and broke in the other.

Think about Total Addressable Content - what are the people in your target audience already watching, reading, reacting to right now? That's your entry point.

**Four growth formats:**
1. **Brandjacking** - Borrow a brand's name recognition, redirect toward your expertise
2. **Newsjacking** - First credible voice to contextualize breaking industry news
3. **Namejacking** - Reference a specific person your audience already follows
4. **Hot takes** - Contrarian position that forces people to pick a side

### Authority Content (30%)

Frameworks, case studies, client results. The difference between "interesting opinions" and "this person can actually help me." Authority content posted to an audience that doesn't know you yet is like handing out a business card in an empty room. Growth gets you seen - authority makes them stay.

### Conversion Content (20%)

You actually have to ask for the business. When you've built trust with growth and authority content, conversion posts feel like a natural next step - not desperation.

### Personal Content (10%)

Makes you memorable. The human behind the expertise. When AI can generate decent content for anyone, the only thing that can't be replicated is a genuine person with a point of view.

## The Comment Flywheel: First 90 Days

In the first 90 days, your comments matter more than your posts.

When you post to zero followers, almost nobody sees it. It's like opening a store on a street with no foot traffic. But when you leave a strong comment on a post hitting 50,000 impressions, you're setting up a pop-up stand on the busiest street in town.

**A good comment does one of three things:**
1. Adds a new angle the original post didn't cover
2. Respectfully challenges the take
3. Tells a quick story that connects to the topic

"Great post" does nothing for you.

**Be strategic:**
- Find posts from people whose audience matches your ICP
- A post with 500 likes from the right people is worth more than 50,000 likes from the wrong ones
- Build a simple engagement list: a spreadsheet with their LinkedIn activity feed link (not profile - that gets creepy)

30 minutes a day on strategic comments before opening your drafts. Do that for 90 days.

Reply to every comment on your own posts, especially in the first hour. More replies means more distribution. These are real people who took 30 seconds to respond - respond back.

## The Honest Timeline

- First 20 posts: practice
- Posts 21-40: where you actually start finding your voice
- Posts 41 and beyond: where momentum kicks in

Don't quit before you get there.
