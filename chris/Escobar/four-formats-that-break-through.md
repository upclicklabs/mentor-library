# Four Formats That Break Through on LinkedIn

We've taken clients from zero to hundreds of thousands of impressions on LinkedIn - not once, repeatedly. Founders nobody had heard of, executives with under 500 followers, people who'd been posting for months with nothing to show for it. Almost every time, it wasn't better writing that changed things. It was the post type.

## Why Most LinkedIn Posts Don't Travel

It's not because they're badly written. It's because they only appeal to a tiny slice of the audience. You write about your niche expertise. The 200 people who already follow you and care about that exact topic see it. Maybe 15 engage. The algorithm reads this as not resonating broadly enough to push further. The post dies.

The posts that break through tap into topics, names, and conversations that a much wider audience within your niche already cares about. When more people read on, more engage. When more engage, the algorithm pushes it harder.

These four formats make your content appeal to the widest possible audience inside your niche - not just the people you already know. You're walking into conversations they are already having.

## Format 1: Brandjacking

Take a decision a well-known brand made - a campaign, product, launch, mistake, pivot - and use it as the frame for your own insight. You are not reporting on the brand. You are borrowing the name recognition they already have and redirecting it toward your area of expertise.

**The mechanism:** When someone sees Heinz, Apple, or Nike in a headline, they already have a reaction before they read a single word. You capture that reaction and steer it.

**Client results:**
- Used Heinz dropping their logo from billboards (still 70% brand recognition without it) to make a point about creative consistency vs. testing velocity in paid social. **9,000 impressions, 193 reactions, 33 saves.**
- Used Liquid Death - broke down how their creative team generates billions in earned media without a traditional ad budget. The VP of Creative at Liquid Death showed up in the comments and corrected a detail. That comment alone got 190 likes.

**The Boomerang Effect:** You throw attention at a brand. When the subject of your post notices and engages, the algorithm reads that as a signal something real is happening. Distribution compounds.

**Rule:** Your insight has to be genuinely yours. Not summarizing their story - using their story as evidence for a point only you would make.

## Format 2: Newsjacking

Being the first credible voice to contextualize something significant happening in your industry. Not summarize it - contextualize it.

**Where to find news before it hits LinkedIn:**
- Google - search trends in your space daily
- LLMs - ask ChatGPT or Claude what's happening in your industry this week
- X (Twitter) - always ahead, where early conversations happen and outliers post first
- Automation - tools like OpenClaw that pull breaking stories before anyone else has posted about them on LinkedIn

**The test:** Can you answer "so what?" in one sentence? If not, you're still summarizing. Keep going until you can.

**Timing is everything.** A sharp take within 24 to 48 hours gets distributed significantly harder than the same post published 4 days later. LinkedIn is always late, so you have a huge advantage.

**Client results:**
- Covered ChatGPT pulling directly from Google search and what that means for SEO strategy: **293 comments, 234 reposts, 28,000 impressions**
- Covered Bing launching an AI performance dashboard: **70 reposts, 40,563 impressions**

## Format 3: Namejacking

Referencing a specific person your audience already follows to stop the scroll, then delivering a perspective that is entirely your own.

More precise than brandjacking - the person you reference usually shares your target audience. Their followers are already your ICP.

**Client results:**
- Referenced Satya Nadella's "know-it-all to learn-it-all culture" at Microsoft - connected to hiring philosophy in growth stage companies: **23,000 impressions**
- Referenced Mira Murati turning down a billion-dollar acquisition from Zuckerberg - connected to what loyalty inside organizations actually looks like: **11,934 impressions**
- Posted about Steven Bartlett - Bartlett himself commented: **147,000 impressions**

When you reference the right person with genuine insight, sometimes that person notices. When they engage with your content, their entire audience sees it. That's distribution you can't buy.

**Rule:** You need a real perspective - not just a summary of what they said. You're adding something, agreeing with nuance, respectfully pushing back, building on it.

## Format 4: Hot Takes

A contrarian position on something your entire industry accepts as true. Not provocative for its own sake. A genuinely held belief that challenges the consensus in a way that forces people to pick a side.

**Why they work:** When someone reads a strong contrarian claim, their brain immediately checks it against what they already believe. They agree or they don't. Either way, they engage.

**Client results:**
- "Original research is outranking AI-written content. Not by a little, by a lot. And that gap is widening." - **390 comments, 300 reposts, 3,198 likes**
- "ChatGPT sold its soul the moment it started testing ads" - **137 comments, 72,000 impressions, still climbing days later**

**The test:** Does it make you slightly nervous to post? If you could post it without any anxiety, it's probably not contrarian enough to move people.

## The Underlying Logic

Every post has a job. The job of these four formats is reach - new people, new audiences. All four do the same thing: they attach your expertise to something that already has an audience paying attention to it.

You're not trying to create attention from scratch. You are redirecting attention that already exists.

**This is the difference between:**
- Standing in an empty room hoping someone walks by
- Walking into a room that's already full

## How to Use Them

These are "growth content" - not your everyday posts. They're designed specifically to bring in new people. Aim for **one to two of these per week**. Mix them with regular authority content. That ratio keeps your profile balanced between reaching new audiences and deepening trust with existing followers.

They also give you something to write about when you're not sure what to say. You don't have to wait for inspiration. Check the news. Look at what brands in your niche are doing. Pay attention to what the people your audience follows are saying. The content is already out there. You just need a real point of view on it.
