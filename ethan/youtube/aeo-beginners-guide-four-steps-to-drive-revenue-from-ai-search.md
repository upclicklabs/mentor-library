## A Reality Check on Google vs AI Search

So, let's start with a reality check. Most self-titled SEO gurus are screaming that Google is dead and that you need to abandon everything and focus only on AI search. And although ChatGPT is the fastest consumer adoption of technology ever, if you look at this data from Spark Toro, Google is handling 13 to 14 billion searches every single day. If you look at OpenAI's own data, the majority of their queries are things like "reformat this email" or "how do I respond to this?"

And the truth is right now people are sticking with what they know — Google. So Google isn't dead, but the tides are beginning to shift. And the point that most marketers or founders are overlooking is that Google itself has now pivoted. More than 50% of searches end with AI overviews.

And this behavioral change is going quietly unnoticed as more and more people want their answers to be served conversationally, not just in keywords. So when 7 billion of Google searches end with AI overviews, you want to create highly citable pieces of content.

The good news is that AEO and GEO are just an additional layer that you need to add to your existing SEO strategy. And all the tactics that I'm about to cover apply across ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, and Google's own AI overviews. All of these are technically now powered by large language models.

So, how do you get your business to be recommended at a specific time that your customer is looking for your product?

## Step 1: Writing Content That AI Search Platforms Will Use

AI search platforms pull the top ranking search results and trusted sources on the web already. They compose answers made up of several different sources. If you take a look at this graph here, you can see that it varies across LLMs. ChatGPT uses different sources compared to Gemini when it generates its answers.

When we used to optimize for SEO, you would structure everything around a specific keyword — "CRM software" for example — and that simply won't work with LLMs. Instead, you need to build it far more conversationally. Structure it around many queries. For example: "What is the best CRM software to help SMEs generate leads?" You're answering an exact question.

Then you want to focus on your headers — H1, H2, H3, and so on — and build the entire piece of content on natural human-like sentence questions. Then your role is to answer that in a very conversational way. In this instance, try and give an expert answer regarding why a certain CRM is better for a small business that wants simplicity and a fast setup.

**Tip:** Download a tool like Whisper Flow or even record a Loom and then talk to your laptop as if you're describing the answer to a friend. This can form the basis of your article and I guarantee it will be written far more naturally than if you didn't do that.

## Step 2: E-E-A-T (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness)

The second thing I want to focus on to make your content work in AI is E-E-A-T. Google has focused on E-E-A-T for years — Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness. AI focuses here very deeply with answers being increased by up to 30% for ChatGPT and Perplexity when brands are including clear sections, expert commentary, and clear FAQs.

How do you create content that adheres to the E-E-A-T principles? It's actually very straightforward and it goes back to my point that AI platforms weigh content with real expert commentary.

E-E-A-T means:

- Sharing real life experiences
- Including expert advice and commentary
- Showcasing case studies and testimonials in the content that you are writing
- Including quotes and statistics from high authority sources or individuals
- Aiming to give multiple perspectives on complex topics
- Using your own content to address known limitations and objections to your product or service

This will allow you to control the narrative in the answers that LLMs provide and give you a positive sentiment when LLMs talk about you.

## Step 3: Semantic Richness and Context

The third thing I want to talk about is how AI chatbots prefer semantic richness and context. Unlike in SEO where we used to repeat the same keyword again and again to try and get it ranked, you want to be using many natural variations explaining the same thing or explaining related concepts around the same topic.

If you're talking about your CRM, you want to have multiple different descriptions and multiple different questions about the CRM rather than just plugging the CRM term.

Key tactics for semantic richness:

- Use clear structures and headings to separate your text and content into really clear, logical sections
- Separate your content into bite-sized question-answer content
- Use bullet points more and lists
- Tables are even shown to improve your ability to be answered — if an AI is asked a very specific question and you have the answer as a header and that specific answer in a bullet point, it's much more likely to mention and cite you and drive traffic towards you because it's easy to retrieve the answer
- Use tables for comparisons of features and for comparisons of pricing — this is an excellent way of passing the information straight through to AI machines

## Step 4: FAQs

This is quite literally gold right now. If an AI is asked a very specific question and you have that exact question as a header with a clear answer underneath, it will probably cite you.

So those are the four points to focus on to really get your content cited by AI.

## Schema Markup for AI

Schema markup is a specialized form of structured data that is used to tell a search engine or AI exactly what content you are showing. It's literally code that machines read that humans don't see.

### Organizational Schema (Foundational)

This tells the AI about your brand, your business, and directs them towards your social channels. In the eyes of AI, this is fundamentally what it sees you as. It's what it knows you as. That is why it's so important to work hard on ensuring these elements are very clear, that your social profiles are set up and posting consistently as this forms your social fortress around your entity — so AI really understands what your business is.

### Article Schema

This is the machine code that is used to communicate the author, the date of publication, the meaning of the article. It's the core element and therefore it's how you're going to be understood by AI. Think of it as labeling your content. It helps AI quickly search through to spot any relevant articles before citing.

**Three quick wins for article schema:**

1. **Attach it to a real expert** — not an admin account. Use a proper author with credentials and a bio. AI trusts people, not anonymous brands.
2. **Mark up the details** — Don't just mark up the page. You need a headline, publish date, last updated, author, and the key entities that you mention.
3. **Keep it fresh** — If you update the article, update the schema. AI systems favor content that's maintained and current.

## Technical Optimization

### HTML Over JavaScript

Ensure that the majority of your content is in HTML rather than in something like JavaScript. AI systems mostly read raw HTML. If it's not in that format, you simply cannot get read by an AI machine right now.

### Images and Videos

AI bots aren't yet very good at capturing images or videos from your website. If you have images or videos, the most important thing to do is to add clear alt text to your images and to add transcriptions to your videos to make it as easy as possible for the AI to understand what those pieces of content actually are.

### Site Map and Site Structure

Ensure that your website's site map and site structure are very clear. A few years ago, there was a trend to push all of your content onto one single page. But these design styles make it extremely confusing for an AI to understand what your site is and what's more important than anything else.

Instead, lay it out in a very logical human hierarchy that helps a crawler to understand your business and what the key content of your website actually is. Make sure that your highly valuable content is not hidden away.

### Crawl Access

Make sure that you're not disallowing Google crawlers, Microsoft Bing, or ChatGPT's bot from actually being allowed to crawl and scrape your website.

To do this, submit site maps in two places:

1. **Google** — still the dominant engine, receives 7 billion AI summaries a day
2. **Bing** — powers ChatGPT

You can update your robots.txt file easily in your website control settings.

There is some controversy around llms.txt files. However, my view is that providing additional context to crawlers cannot hurt the outcome as to whether you are answered within a chat service.

## Building Authority Off-Site

AI models don't just read your website. They're trained on the entirety of the internet. The sites that form the backbone of the training of the AI models carry enormous weight in how they think and answer. Wikipedia, Reddit, Quora, Crunchbase, Yelp, Google Maps — these are primary training data sources. Getting your business accurately listed and described in these places doesn't just help humans find you. It literally shapes how AI models understand what your company is and what it does.

### 1. User-Generated Content

Places like Reddit, Quora, and other forums have millions of users and conversations happening every day and are heavily prioritized and used by LLMs to formulate answers.

Don't just set up a fake account and spam your ideal messaging into these forums. Find threads that really matter and reply with things like: "I work at X company and based on your specific understanding of the problem and the use case, here is how you fix it. Here is how you change it and here's how I can help you do that."

The point is you're trying to give context to a user who has asked that specific question because that informs how an LLM will think about you and it will inform the sentiment that the LLM has about your brand.

### 2. Large Knowledge Bases

LLMs are very focused on large reputable and established knowledge sources like Wikipedia, Yelp, Maps, and Crunchbase. These are the primary training data for most of the models. You should focus on getting your business listed and described in these knowledge bases as this will strengthen your entity — the entity that the models are trying to understand — and it will make it more likely that you are mentioned and more likely that you are cited.

### 3. Publications and Digital PR

You as a business want reputable publications to write about your brand, to mention your name, and to cite you. When an LLM is considering an answer, they consider large publications as authoritative sources on that subject matter. If those sources are talking about you in the context of being an authority and the context of being an answer to the question, you are more likely to show up as an answer.

The advice: reach out to journalists, offer comments, form a differentiated opinion. Create highly citable pieces of content. For example, at Searchable, we created the "State of AI SEO" algorithm report, analyzing millions of data points and giving objective data on how the models are working and creating answers. Dozens of journalists want to cite this content when they're talking about the evolution of technology and the evolution of consumer behavior, resulting in lots of different links back, which increases our knowledge entity in the AI machine databases, meaning we're more likely to get mentioned and cited in future for topics around AEO.

This is what we call digital PR or link building. You're trying to get your comments and your content to be cited by small and large publications alike. The more established and the more authoritative, the better — from niche blogs through to international newspapers. The more novel and on-trend that your content is, the more likely it is to get cited.

Reach out to journalists, build relationships, and give them something to write about. This is the new era of PR or digital PR. Earn citations, earn authority, and you will go on to earn traffic.
