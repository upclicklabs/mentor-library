# The How-To vs How-I Content Shift

The content that built most LinkedIn brands is becoming worthless.

For years, the playbook was clear: teach people how to do things. Share frameworks. Give step-by-step instructions. Position yourself as the expert. That worked. It built audiences. It built businesses. But it's not working the same way anymore.

## What Changed

AI can now give people personalized how-to advice instantly. Someone wants to know how to write a LinkedIn hook - they can ask ChatGPT and get a decent answer in seconds, tailored to their industry, tailored to their situation. That tutorial you spent 3 hours writing is competing with infinite free personalized instruction.

Jay Claus (built a massive audience on LinkedIn and YouTube through educational content) said it publicly: "How-to instructional content is still useful, but it's far less valuable now because it's easier to just prompt an AI about your specific situation and get personalized guidance in moments."

## The Shift: From How-To to How-I

**How-to content** positions you as a teacher standing above your audience - AI can easily replicate it. If they need the instructions, there's always a way to search for it.

**How-I content** positions you as figuring things out alongside your audience. You're sharing your learning as you learn it. You're inviting them into your processes. You're not lecturing from a stage - and you're positioning yourself as someone credible to say so.

Think about the people you actually trust online. The creators whose content you'd never skip. They're not the ones giving generic frameworks - they're the ones showing you their actual thinking, their real decisions, their honest results, including the failures.

## Five Types of Content People Still Seek Out

None of them are tutorials:
1. **Long-form writing** where people connect dots between different ideas in ways that feel original
2. **Effortful work** where you can tell someone put real time and skill into making something
3. **Demonstrations** where people show their actual processes, not just explain concepts
4. **Interviews** with people who have unique, verifiable experiences
5. **Good hangs** - people who just make you feel comfortable and at ease

Notice what's missing: step-by-step instructions with zero personality. Seven ways to do something. That stuff has its place, but it doesn't create connection and doesn't make you trustworthy.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

Instead of: "How to write a LinkedIn hook that stops the scroll"
Try: "I tested 47 hooks last month. Here's what I learned about my audience."

Instead of: "The five-step framework for client onboarding"
Try: "We changed our onboarding process after losing a client. Here's what we do differently."

Instead of: "How to price your services"
Try: "I raised my prices 40% and lost two clients. Here's why I'd do it again."

One is generic advice anyone could give. The other is a real story only you can tell. One positions you as an expert dispensing wisdom. The other positions you as a practitioner sharing your journey. **One can be replaced by AI. The other cannot.**

## Why Vulnerability Works

How-I requires vulnerability - and that's exactly why it works.

Jay also said: "I feel less confident and less qualified to teach right now because things are changing so fast... I'll be embodying the role of a teacher less in this season. Right now, I feel like I'm back to being a student, a friend on a parallel journey."

That's not weakness. That's honesty. And it creates more trust, not less.

This is the shift from **guru to guide** - from expert to fellow traveler. Counterintuitively, this positioning is more powerful right now than the "I have all the answers" approach ever was. Because everyone is figuring this out. The landscape is changing fast. Anyone who claims to have it all figured out sounds out of touch.

## The Strategic Reason: AI-Proof Content

How-I content is AI-proof. How-to content is not.

LinkedIn is now the second most cited source in AI overviews. Your content isn't just competing for attention in the feed - it's potentially training the AI systems that will make recommendations to your future buyers.

Generic how-to content gets absorbed and summarized. Your frameworks become everyone's frameworks.

But how-I content that becomes a citation, a source, a specific example that AI references because it's unique to you - you become the case study, not the commodity.

## How to Make the Shift

1. **Start documenting more than instructing.** The next time you solve a problem for a client or make a decision in your business, write about what you actually did, why you did it, and what happened.

2. **Share your learning in real time.** You don't need to have figured something out before you post about it. Some of the best content comes from being in the middle of a challenge and sharing what you're discovering along the way.

3. **Include the failures.** Your wins are important, but your losses are what make you relatable. Everyone is tired of people who only show the highlight reel. The mess is where trust gets built.

4. **Show your actual work.** Don't just explain your process - demonstrate it. Screen share. Show the document. Walk through the real example. Let people see how you actually think, not just hear you describe it.

## The Core Insight

Information used to be scarce. If you had knowledge other people didn't have, you could build a brand just by sharing it.

Information is abundant now. Anyone can get the how-to in seconds.

**What's scarce now is trust, real experience, a genuine point of view earned through actually doing the work.**

The how-to era of content isn't completely dead. Very specific instructional content still has a place. But if that's all you're creating, you're playing a game you can't win anymore.

The thought leaders who will win the next few years are the ones who shift from how-to to how-I - from "here's what you should do" to "here's what I'm doing and learning." That's the trust shift. And it's already happening.
